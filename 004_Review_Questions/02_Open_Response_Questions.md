## Open-Ended Questions

### Introduction to NLP

#### Question 1: Paradigm Shifts in NLP

- **(A)** Describe the major paradigm shifts in the historical evolution of NLP, from rule-based systems to statistical models and finally to neural and transformer-based approaches.

**Answer:** NLP evolved through three major paradigms: (1) Rule-based systems (1950s-1980s) used hand-crafted grammars and expert knowledge; (2) Statistical methods (1990s-2000s) learned patterns from data using probabilistic models like HMMs and n-grams; (3) Neural approaches (2010s+) used deep learning with word embeddings, RNNs, and Transformers to learn hierarchical representations end-to-end.

- **(B)** Analyze why each transition occurred, focusing on the limitations of the previous paradigm and the roles of computational power and data availability.

**Answer:** Rule-based systems failed to scale due to linguistic complexity and brittleness. Statistical models emerged with more data and compute but suffered from feature engineering and sparsity. Neural methods succeeded when massive datasets, GPU acceleration, and architectural innovations (attention, pretraining) enabled learning rich representations directly from data without manual feature design.

#### Question 2: Ambiguity and the Hardness of NLP

- **(A)** Explain why ambiguity is a fundamental challenge in natural language processing and how syntactic ambiguity can grow exponentially with sentence complexity.

**Answer:** Ambiguity is fundamental because natural language permits multiple valid interpretations of the same input—lexically (word senses), syntactically (parse trees), and semantically (meaning). Syntactic ambiguity grows exponentially because each prepositional phrase, modifier, or coordination structure can attach to multiple constituents, creating a combinatorial explosion: with n ambiguous attachments, the number of valid parse trees can approach Catalan numbers O(4^n/n^(3/2)), making exhaustive enumeration intractable without probabilistic models to rank interpretations.

- **(B)** Compare natural languages with formal computer languages in terms of ambiguity, determinism, and interpretability, and discuss the implications for computational modeling.

**Answer:** Natural languages are inherently ambiguous, context-dependent, and lack explicit formal specifications, requiring probabilistic models and world knowledge for disambiguation. Formal languages are unambiguous by design, deterministic in evaluation, and fully specified by explicit grammars, enabling deterministic parsers. This means NLP systems must use statistical learning and neural approximation to handle ambiguity and incompleteness, while compilers leverage precise syntax for guaranteed correctness—making NLP fundamentally harder and requiring data-driven rather than rule-based approaches.

#### Question 3: Task Taxonomy in NLP

- **(A)** Define and distinguish between syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic NLP tasks.

**Answer:** Syntactic NLP tasks focus on the grammatical structure and form of language, including parsing, part-of-speech tagging, and constituency/dependency tree construction—essentially analyzing how words are arranged according to grammatical rules. Semantic tasks address the meaning of linguistic expressions, such as word sense disambiguation, semantic role labeling, and entity linking, determining what sentences mean independent of context. Pragmatic tasks interpret language in context, including coreference resolution, sentiment analysis, and dialogue act classification, capturing how meaning depends on speaker intent, discourse context, and real-world knowledge.

- **(B)** Analyze how these task categories interact in a complete language understanding pipeline, and explain why syntactic processing alone is insufficient for full interpretation.

**Answer:** In a complete language understanding pipeline, these categories form a hierarchical dependency: syntactic analysis provides structural scaffolding that semantic interpretation builds upon, while pragmatics resolves ambiguities and interprets meaning within broader context. Syntactic processing alone is insufficient because structure doesn't determine meaning—syntactically identical sentences can have different meanings (e.g., "The duck is ready to eat" where "duck" is subject vs. object), and pragmatic factors like sarcasm, implicature, and world knowledge fundamentally alter interpretation beyond what syntax and semantics alone can capture. Full understanding requires integrating all three levels to resolve ambiguity, ground references, and infer communicative intent.

### Word Embeddings and Distributional Semantics

#### Question 1: Limitations of Discrete Representations and the Distributional Hypothesis

- **(A)** Formally explain why one-hot word representations are orthogonal and why this property prevents the encoding of semantic similarity.

**Answer:** One-hot word representations are orthogonal because each word is represented as a vector with exactly one dimension set to 1 (corresponding to that word's index in the vocabulary) and all other dimensions set to 0. This encoding scheme ensures that for any two distinct words w₁ and w₂, their one-hot vectors v₁ and v₂ satisfy v₁ᵀv₂ = 0, making them orthogonal. This orthogonality prevents encoding semantic similarity because the inner product (and consequently cosine similarity) between any two different words is always zero, regardless of whether they are semantically related (like "cat" and "dog") or completely unrelated (like "cat" and "astronomy"). Since geometric measures of similarity like cosine similarity rely on non-zero inner products to quantify relatedness, the uniform orthogonality of one-hot vectors means that all words are equidistant from each other in the representation space, making it impossible to distinguish between semantically similar and dissimilar word pairs.

- **(B)** Describe how the distributional hypothesis addresses these limitations and analyze how dense word embeddings induce a geometric notion of graded similarity.

**Answer:** The distributional hypothesis, which states that "words that occur in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings," addresses the limitations of one-hot representations by enabling the learning of dense, low-dimensional word embeddings where semantic relationships are encoded geometrically. Instead of assigning arbitrary, orthogonal vectors to words, distributional methods (like Word2Vec or GloVe) learn embeddings by observing word co-occurrence patterns in large corpora—words that appear in similar contexts (e.g., "cat" and "dog" both appearing near words like "pet," "feed," "walk") will have their embedding vectors pushed closer together in the learned space. These dense embeddings induce a geometric notion of graded similarity because semantically related words end up with high cosine similarity (vectors pointing in similar directions), while unrelated words have low or negative similarity. This continuous similarity measure enables the representation to capture fine-grained semantic relationships, analogical reasoning (e.g., king - man + woman ≈ queen), and generalization across semantically similar words, fundamentally solving the rigidity of discrete one-hot representations.

#### Question 2: Word2Vec Objective and Optimization

- **(A)** Derive the predictive objective of the Word2Vec model, including the softmax formulation of $P(o \mid c)$ and its relationship to cross-entropy loss.

**Answer:** The Word2Vec Skip-Gram model learns embeddings by predicting context words given a center word, defining the conditional probability as $P(o \mid c) = \frac{\exp(u_o^T v_c)}{\sum_{w \in V} \exp(u_w^T v_c)}$, where $v_c$ is the center word embedding, $u_o$ is the context embedding, and the softmax normalizes over the entire vocabulary V. The training objective maximizes log-likelihood: $J = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{-m \leq j \leq m, j \neq 0} \log P(w_{t+j} \mid w_t)$, which is equivalent to minimizing cross-entropy loss between the predicted distribution and the true one-hot distribution over observed context words—since cross-entropy reduces to $-\log P(o_{true} \mid c)$ for one-hot targets, Word2Vec directly applies cross-entropy minimization to push high probability mass onto actually observed word-context pairs while the softmax denominator implicitly lowers probability for unobserved pairs.

- **(B)** Explain why stochastic gradient descent is necessary for scalability and analyze how gradient-based optimization leads to emergent semantic structure in the embedding space.

**Answer:** Stochastic gradient descent is necessary because computing the full softmax gradient requires O(|V|) operations per training step (summing over all vocabulary words in $\sum_{w \in V} P(w \mid c) u_w$), making training on billion-word corpora prohibitively expensive, whereas SGD with negative sampling reduces complexity to O(k) by sampling only k≈5-20 negative examples per update, enabling practical training in hours rather than weeks. Gradient-based optimization produces emergent semantic structure because words appearing in similar contexts (like "cat" and "dog" near "pet," "feed") receive similar gradient updates that push their embeddings closer together—specifically, the dot product $u_o^T v_c$ in the objective rewards alignment of co-occurring word pairs while the softmax creates competition that separates non-co-occurring pairs, causing semantically related words to cluster geometrically through accumulated gradient updates, with analogical relationships like "king" - "man" + "woman" ≈ "queen" emerging naturally as parallel vector offsets that encode systematic meaning dimensions learned implicitly from distributional patterns rather than being explicitly programmed.

#### Question 3: Skip-Gram vs CBOW and Training Dynamics

- **(A)** Compare the Skip-Gram and CBOW architectures in terms of prediction direction, training behavior, and sensitivity to rare versus frequent words.

**Answer:** Skip-Gram and CBOW represent complementary prediction directions in Word2Vec: Skip-Gram predicts context words given a center word (one-to-many), while CBOW predicts the center word from surrounding context words (many-to-one). This architectural difference creates distinct training behaviors—Skip-Gram treats each context position as a separate training example, generating multiple updates per center word and thus providing more training signal, which makes it particularly effective for rare words that appear infrequently but in diverse contexts. CBOW, by contrast, averages context embeddings before prediction, smoothing over positional variations and emphasizing the aggregate distributional pattern, which makes it faster to train and more effective for frequent words where abundant data supports stable averaging, but causes it to underrepresent rare words whose limited occurrences get diluted in the averaging process. Consequently, Skip-Gram exhibits higher sensitivity to rare words and captures finer-grained semantic distinctions, while CBOW trains faster and performs better on high-frequency words where statistical averaging improves robustness.

- **(B)** Analyze how the end-to-end training pipeline, including vocabulary construction, context window extraction, embedding lookup, and parameter updates, enables word vectors to capture distributional meaning.

**Answer:** The end-to-end Word2Vec training pipeline enables embeddings to capture distributional meaning through a coordinated sequence of operations that directly optimize for contextual co-occurrence patterns. First, vocabulary construction establishes the discrete token space and assigns each word a learnable embedding vector. Then, context window extraction (e.g., sliding a window of size 5) generates training pairs that operationalize the distributional hypothesis by linking each word to its immediate lexical neighborhood. During forward propagation, embedding lookup retrieves the relevant vectors, which are then passed through a shallow neural network (either projecting center embeddings to predict context in Skip-Gram, or aggregating context embeddings to predict the center in CBOW) to compute prediction scores via dot products followed by softmax. The cross-entropy loss measures how well the model predicts observed co-occurrences, and backpropagation computes gradients that flow back through the network to update both the embedding matrix and output weights. Through iterative stochastic gradient descent over millions of such context windows, embeddings for words appearing in similar contexts (e.g., "cat" and "dog" both co-occurring with "pet," "feed") receive similar gradient updates that push their vectors closer together in the shared embedding space, while unrelated words diverge—thus geometrically encoding the distributional hypothesis where semantic similarity emerges directly from patterns of contextual usage learned end-to-end from raw text.

### Neural Training and Optimization

#### Question 1: Optimization and Negative Sampling

- **(A)** Explain why the full softmax objective in Skip-Gram is computationally expensive and how stochastic gradient descent enables scalable training.

**Answer:** The full softmax objective in Skip-Gram is computationally expensive because computing P(o|c) = exp(u_o^T v_c) / Σ_w exp(u_w^T v_c) requires normalizing over the entire vocabulary V at every training step, making each gradient computation O(|V|) where |V| can be hundreds of thousands of words. For a corpus with millions of training pairs, this becomes prohibitively expensive. Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) enables scalable training by processing one (or mini-batches of) context-target pairs at a time rather than computing gradients over the entire dataset, which dramatically reduces memory requirements and allows incremental updates. By randomly sampling training examples and updating parameters immediately, SGD provides noisy but unbiased gradient estimates that converge efficiently while avoiding the need to load the entire corpus into memory or perform full-vocabulary summations repeatedly, making Word2Vec training practical even on large corpora with commodity hardware.

- **(B)** Derive the intuition behind skip-gram with negative sampling and analyze how replacing multiclass prediction with binary classification preserves semantic learning while improving efficiency.

**Answer:** Skip-gram with negative sampling (SGNS) reformulates the expensive multiclass softmax prediction into multiple independent binary classification problems: instead of predicting which word appears in context from all vocabulary words, it asks "does word o actually appear near word c?" (positive example) versus "does random word w_neg appear near c?" (negative example), training the model to distinguish true context pairs from randomly sampled noise using sigmoid binary classifiers. This preserves semantic learning because words appearing in similar contexts still receive similar gradient updates—true context pairs (c, o_true) push their dot products u_o^T v_c higher, while negative samples push unrelated pairs apart, effectively implementing the distributional hypothesis by making contextually similar words cluster together while separating unrelated words. Efficiency improves dramatically because instead of computing O(|V|) softmax normalization per example, SGNS only computes k+1 sigmoid evaluations (one positive, k negatives, typically k=5-20), reducing complexity from O(|V|) to O(k), enabling training on billion-word corpora in hours while maintaining the fundamental principle that semantic similarity emerges from co-occurrence patterns encoded geometrically in the learned embedding space.

#### Question 2: Count-Based vs Predictive Embeddings

- **(A)** Compare count-based representations using co-occurrence matrices and dimensionality reduction (e.g., SVD) with direct prediction methods such as Word2Vec.

**Answer:** Count-based representations construct explicit co-occurrence matrices that capture global corpus statistics by counting how frequently words appear near each other within fixed context windows, then apply dimensionality reduction techniques like Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) or Positive Pointwise Mutual Information (PPMI) weighting to produce dense, low-dimensional embeddings that preserve the major patterns of co-occurrence while reducing sparsity and noise. In contrast, direct prediction methods like Word2Vec (Skip-Gram and CBOW) optimize neural network parameters to predict context words from center words (or vice versa) using local training objectives, learning embeddings implicitly through backpropagation as the model adjusts weights to maximize predictive accuracy on word-context pairs sampled from the corpus. The fundamental difference is that count-based methods explicitly compute and decompose global statistics in a two-stage process (count then factor), while predictive methods learn embeddings end-to-end by iteratively updating parameters based on local prediction errors, with the distributional hypothesis emerging implicitly through the optimization process rather than being explicitly encoded in a co-occurrence matrix.

- **(B)** Analyze the trade-offs between these approaches in terms of computational efficiency, representational expressiveness, and downstream task performance.

**Answer:** The trade-offs between these approaches reflect competing priorities in computational efficiency, representational expressiveness, and task performance. Count-based methods with SVD benefit from leveraging global corpus statistics and deterministic matrix factorization, making them reproducible and potentially more stable, but they require constructing and storing massive sparse matrices (O(|V|²) entries for vocabulary size V) and performing expensive SVD operations (O(min(|V|², nd²)) for n documents and d dimensions), which becomes prohibitively costly for large vocabularies. Predictive methods like Word2Vec scale more gracefully through stochastic online learning with negative sampling, requiring only O(k) computations per training example (where k is the number of negative samples, typically 5-20) and enabling incremental updates without materializing full co-occurrence matrices, though they introduce hyperparameter sensitivity (learning rate, window size, negative sample count) and non-deterministic convergence due to random initialization and sampling. In terms of representational expressiveness, recent analyses (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) show that Word2Vec with negative sampling implicitly factorizes a shifted PMI matrix, suggesting theoretical equivalence, though predictive methods often capture more nuanced semantic relationships and perform better on analogy tasks and downstream applications, possibly because the iterative optimization with sampling acts as implicit regularization that prevents overfitting to noisy co-occurrences, while count-based methods can be sensitive to hyperparameters like smoothing, weighting schemes, and the number of SVD dimensions retained—ultimately, modern practice favors predictive methods for their computational scalability and strong empirical performance, though hybrid approaches like GloVe combine global statistics (like count-based methods) with local context prediction objectives to merge the strengths of both paradigms.

#### Question 3: Evaluation and Word Sense Ambiguity

- **(A)** Distinguish between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation of word embeddings and discuss their respective strengths and limitations.

**Answer:** Intrinsic evaluation measures word embeddings directly through tasks like word similarity (comparing cosine similarity to human judgments on pairs like "cat"/"dog"), analogy completion (solving "king - man + woman ≈ queen"), and clustering quality, offering fast, interpretable assessments of whether embeddings capture semantic relationships without requiring downstream task infrastructure—however, these evaluations may not correlate strongly with real-world performance since optimizing for similarity judgments doesn't guarantee utility in practical applications. Extrinsic evaluation assesses embeddings indirectly by measuring performance on downstream NLP tasks like sentiment analysis, named entity recognition, or machine translation, where embeddings serve as input features—this approach directly measures practical utility and end-to-end effectiveness but is expensive (requiring full task pipelines), confounds embedding quality with task-specific factors (architecture, hyperparameters, training data), and provides less interpretable feedback about what semantic properties the embeddings capture, making it harder to diagnose specific representational deficiencies.

- **(B)** Explain why static embeddings struggle with word sense ambiguity and analyze how contextual or multi-representation approaches address this limitation.

**Answer:** Static embeddings struggle with word sense ambiguity because they assign each word type a single fixed vector regardless of context, forcing polysemous words like "bank" (financial institution vs. river edge) to collapse all senses into one averaged representation that dilutes distinct meanings and fails to capture sense-specific semantic relationships—for instance, the static "bank" vector will be equidistant from both "money" and "river," preventing accurate disambiguation. Contextual embeddings (like ELMo, BERT, GPT) address this limitation by generating dynamic, context-dependent representations where the same word receives different vectors based on surrounding tokens—"bank" in "river bank" produces a vector close to "shore" while "bank" in "savings bank" aligns with "finance"—effectively performing implicit word sense disambiguation through attention mechanisms that weight context appropriately. Multi-representation approaches like multi-prototype embeddings explicitly learn multiple vectors per word type (one per sense cluster discovered through unsupervised methods), allowing direct modeling of polysemy, though these require sense disambiguation mechanisms and may not scale as gracefully as fully contextual models that handle disambiguation implicitly through infinite context-dependent representations.

### Window-Based Models and Neural Classification

#### Question 1: Window-Based Modeling and Margin Objectives

- **(A)** Explain how a window-based formulation reduces NLP tasks such as Named Entity Recognition to local classification problems and describe the modeling assumptions involved.

**Answer:** Window-based formulation reduces NLP tasks like Named Entity Recognition to local classification by extracting fixed-size context windows around each target token (e.g., 2 words left and right) and treating each window as an independent classification instance—for example, deciding whether the center word is a location entity based only on its immediate neighbors. The key modeling assumptions are: (1) local sufficiency: all information needed for classification is contained within the fixed window, ignoring long-range dependencies; (2) context independence: each window is classified independently without considering interactions between adjacent predictions; and (3) fixed receptive field: the window size is predetermined and uniform across all instances, potentially missing relevant context that falls outside the window boundaries. This transforms a structured sequence labeling problem into a collection of local binary or multiclass classification decisions that can be trained efficiently using standard neural architectures.

- **(B)** Derive the max-margin hinge loss used to separate correct and corrupted windows, and analyze how margin-based learning differs from probabilistic objectives.

**Answer** The max-margin hinge loss is derived by pairing each correct window (with score S for a true entity mention) against corrupted windows (with scores S_c for negative examples where the center token is not the target entity type) and defining the loss as J = max(0, 1 - S + S_c), which penalizes the model whenever the correct window's score fails to exceed the corrupted window's score by at least a margin of 1. Margin-based learning differs fundamentally from probabilistic objectives (like cross-entropy with softmax) by focusing on relative score separation rather than absolute probability calibration—it only cares that correct examples score higher than incorrect ones by a safety margin, allowing confident predictions (S >> S_c) to contribute zero loss, whereas probabilistic objectives always apply non-zero loss by measuring divergence from the true probability distribution, continuously pushing probabilities toward 0 or 1. This makes margin-based methods more robust to outliers and less sensitive to probability miscalibration, though they sacrifice probabilistic interpretability and may struggle with uncertainty quantification compared to likelihood-based approaches.

#### Question 2: Neural Scoring and Backpropagation

- **(A)** Describe how a neural scoring function maps concatenated word embeddings from a context window to a scalar compatibility score.

**Answer:** A neural scoring function maps concatenated word embeddings from a context window to a scalar compatibility score through a multi-layer feedforward architecture: given a context window of size 2k+1 centered on a target word, the embeddings for all words in the window (each of dimension d) are concatenated into a single vector of dimension (2k+1)×d, which is then passed through one or more hidden layers with non-linear activations (typically ReLU or tanh) to extract compositional features, and finally projected through a linear output layer to produce an unnormalized scalar score representing the compatibility or likelihood that the center word belongs to a particular class (e.g., being a location entity in NER). This architecture enables the model to learn non-linear combinations of the contextual embeddings, capturing patterns like "words appearing near 'in' and 'located' are likely locations," with the score used directly in margin-based objectives or normalized via softmax for probabilistic classification.

- **(B)** Explain how backpropagation computes parameter updates in this multi-layer network and analyze how gradients flow through the computational graph during training.

**Answer:** Backpropagation computes parameter updates by applying the chain rule to propagate gradients backward through the computational graph from the loss function to all parameters: starting with the loss gradient ∂J/∂s (where s is the output score), gradients flow backward through the output layer to compute ∂J/∂W_out and ∂J/∂h (where h is the hidden layer activation), then through the non-linear activation function using its local derivative (e.g., ∂tanh/∂z = 1 - tanh²(z)), continuing backward through hidden layers to compute ∂J/∂W_hidden, and finally reaching the embedding layer where ∂J/∂E updates the word vectors themselves. During training, gradients accumulate differently across the computational graph: parameters in later layers (closer to the loss) receive direct gradient signals and update quickly, while gradients flowing to embeddings pass through multiple matrix multiplications and non-linearities, potentially becoming diluted—this hierarchical gradient flow means that the network learns to compose local contextual patterns in hidden layers while simultaneously fine-tuning word embeddings to encode distributional semantics that support these compositional operations, with stochastic gradient descent enabling efficient parameter updates by processing mini-batches of context windows rather than the entire corpus.

#### Question 3: Optimization Dynamics and Embedding Fine-Tuning

- **(A)** Explain how stochastic gradient descent enables scalable optimization in neural NLP models trained over large corpora.

**Answer:** Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) enables scalable optimization in neural NLP models by processing one training example (or small mini-batches) at a time rather than computing gradients over the entire corpus, which dramatically reduces memory requirements from O(N) to O(1) where N is the corpus size and allows parameter updates to begin immediately without waiting for a full pass through billions of tokens. Unlike batch gradient descent which requires loading the entire dataset and computing exact gradients—prohibitively expensive for corpora with millions of documents—SGD samples random training instances (e.g., individual word-context pairs in Word2Vec or sentences in language modeling), computes noisy but unbiased gradient estimates using backpropagation, and updates parameters incrementally with each example, effectively turning optimization into an online learning process. This stochastic sampling introduces gradient noise that actually helps escape local minima and provides implicit regularization, while the frequent parameter updates (potentially millions per epoch) enable faster convergence than waiting for full-batch gradients, making it feasible to train neural models with hundreds of millions of parameters on web-scale corpora using standard hardware—ultimately, SGD's O(k) computational complexity per update (where k is mini-batch size, typically 32-256) versus O(N) for batch methods is what makes modern deep learning for NLP computationally tractable.

- **(B)** Analyze the trade-offs involved in fine-tuning pretrained word embeddings during task-specific training, particularly with respect to overfitting and semantic drift.

**Answer:** Fine-tuning pretrained word embeddings during task-specific training involves critical trade-offs between task specialization and semantic integrity: when training data is abundant (e.g., >100k examples), fine-tuning allows embeddings to adapt to task-specific distributional patterns, potentially improving performance by adjusting "dog" to be closer to "aggressive" in sentiment analysis of product reviews where negative dog-related mentions dominate, but this risks overfitting when datasets are small since the limited task data provides insufficient signal to reliably update high-dimensional embeddings (e.g., 300 dimensions × 50k vocabulary = 15M parameters), causing the model to memorize spurious training correlations rather than learning generalizable patterns. Additionally, fine-tuning causes semantic drift where task-specific updates corrupt the rich, general-purpose semantic relationships learned from billions of tokens during pretraining—for instance, adjusting embeddings for a medical NER task might push "doctor" away from "physician" if the training set happens to use only one term, degrading the embedding's utility for transfer to other tasks and breaking useful analogical relationships (king - man + woman ≈ queen) that rely on stable geometric structure. The optimal strategy depends on dataset size: freeze embeddings for small datasets (<10k examples) to prevent overfitting and preserve pretrained semantics, fine-tune with small learning rates (10x smaller than other parameters) for medium datasets to allow gentle adaptation while maintaining general structure, and fully fine-tune for large datasets where abundant signal justifies task-specific specialization—modern practice often uses hybrid approaches like adapter layers or low-rank adaptation (LoRA) that add task-specific parameters while keeping pretrained embeddings frozen, achieving specialization without semantic corruption.

#### Question 2: Neural Scoring and Gradient Flow

- **(A)** Describe how a feed-forward neural network computes an unnormalized compatibility score for a context window.

**Answer:** A feed-forward neural network computes an unnormalized compatibility score for a context window by first concatenating the word embeddings of all tokens within the window (e.g., 2k+1 words for a window of size k) into a single high-dimensional input vector of size (2k+1)×d, where d is the embedding dimension. This concatenated vector is then passed through one or more hidden layers with non-linear activation functions (typically ReLU or tanh) that learn to compose and extract higher-level features from the local context, followed by a final linear projection layer that produces a scalar score representing the compatibility or likelihood that the center word belongs to a particular class (e.g., being a location entity in Named Entity Recognition). This score remains unnormalized—it's a raw logit value that can be used directly in margin-based objectives or normalized via softmax for probabilistic classification—and the network learns to assign higher scores to valid context-label combinations through gradient-based optimization.

- **(B)** Explain how backpropagation operates over the computational graph to update parameters, including the role of upstream and local gradients.

**Answer:** Backpropagation operates over the computational graph by applying the chain rule to propagate error gradients backward from the loss function through each layer to all learnable parameters. Starting with the loss gradient ∂J/∂s (where s is the output score), gradients flow backward through the output layer to compute ∂J/∂W_out and ∂J/∂h (where h is the hidden activation), then through the non-linear activation using its local derivative (e.g., ∂ReLU/∂z = 1 if z>0, else 0), continuing through hidden layers to compute ∂J/∂W_hidden, and finally reaching the embedding layer to update word vectors via ∂J/∂E. At each layer, the gradient with respect to a parameter is computed as the product of the upstream gradient (flowing from layers closer to the loss) and the local gradient (the derivative of that layer's operation with respect to its parameters), enabling the network to adjust all parameters—from output weights to word embeddings—in directions that reduce the loss. This hierarchical gradient flow allows the network to simultaneously learn compositional feature extractors in hidden layers while fine-tuning word embeddings to encode contextual patterns that support the classification objective.

#### Question 3: Embedding Fine-Tuning and Optimization Trade-offs

- **(A)** Explain the risks associated with retraining pre-trained word embeddings during task-specific training.

**Answer:** Retraining pre-trained word embeddings during task-specific training carries significant risks, primarily overfitting and semantic drift. When training data is limited (e.g., <10k examples), fine-tuning high-dimensional embeddings (typically 300 dimensions × tens of thousands of vocabulary = millions of parameters) provides insufficient signal to reliably update the weights, causing the model to memorize spurious training correlations rather than learning generalizable patterns. Additionally, task-specific updates can corrupt the rich, general-purpose semantic relationships learned from billions of tokens during pretraining—for example, adjusting embeddings for a domain-specific sentiment task might push "excellent" away from "great" if the training set happens to use them in different contexts, breaking useful analogical relationships (king - man + woman ≈ queen) and degrading the embedding's utility for transfer to other tasks or domains.

- **(B)** Analyze how dataset size influences the decision to fine-tune embeddings and discuss the trade-off between task specialization and global semantic consistency.

**Answer:** Dataset size critically influences fine-tuning decisions through a trade-off between task specialization and semantic consistency: with small datasets (<10k examples), embeddings should remain frozen to preserve pretrained semantic structure and prevent overfitting; medium datasets (10k-100k examples) benefit from fine-tuning with reduced learning rates (typically 10× smaller than other parameters) to allow gentle task-specific adaptation while maintaining general semantic relationships; large datasets (>100k examples) provide sufficient signal to justify full fine-tuning where task specialization outweighs the risk of semantic drift. The fundamental tension is that task-specific fine-tuning improves performance by adapting embeddings to domain-specific distributional patterns (e.g., making "bank" closer to "finance" in a banking corpus), but this specialization comes at the cost of corrupting global semantic consistency learned from massive pretraining corpora, potentially reducing cross-task transfer and breaking compositional reasoning—modern practice increasingly favors hybrid approaches like adapter layers or LoRA that add task-specific parameters while keeping pretrained embeddings frozen, achieving specialization without semantic corruption.

### Syntactic Parsing

#### Question 1: Phrase Structure and Context-Free Grammars

- **(A)** Explain how phrase structure encodes hierarchical organization in sentences and describe how context-free grammars (CFGs) formalize this structure using production rules.

**Answer:** Phrase structure encodes hierarchical organization by grouping words into nested constituents (like noun phrases, verb phrases) that function as single units within larger structures, representing the intuition that sentences aren't flat sequences but rather tree-like compositions where phrases can contain sub-phrases. Context-free grammars formalize this structure through production rules of the form A → β, where a non-terminal symbol A (like NP or VP) expands into a sequence β of terminals (words) and/or non-terminals, enabling systematic generation and recognition of grammatically valid sentences—for example, S → NP VP, NP → Det N, VP → V NP recursively define how a sentence decomposes into subject and predicate, which further decompose into determiner-noun and verb-object structures, directly encoding the hierarchical phrase relationships through rewrite rules that mirror the tree structure of syntactic constituency.

- **(B)** Analyze how recursion in CFGs enables nested constructions and discuss limitations of CFG-based representations in handling structural ambiguity.

**Answer:** Recursion in CFGs enables nested constructions by allowing non-terminals to appear in their own expansions (directly or indirectly), such as NP → NP PP allowing "the cat on the mat in the house" to embed prepositional phrases indefinitely, or S → S Conj S enabling coordinate structures like "John ran and Mary walked and Tom jumped" with arbitrary depth—this recursive capacity captures the creative, unbounded nature of language where finite rules generate infinite sentences. However, CFG-based representations struggle with structural ambiguity because a single sentence can have multiple valid parse trees (e.g., "I saw the man with the telescope" where the PP "with the telescope" can attach to either the VP or the inner NP), and standard CFGs provide no principled mechanism to rank these alternatives without augmentation through probabilistic weighting (PCFGs), lexical preferences, or semantic constraints, making disambiguation require external information beyond the context-free formalism itself, which treats all derivations satisfying the grammar as equally valid regardless of plausibility or meaning.

#### Question 2: Structural Ambiguity and Parsing Decisions

- **(A)** Describe at least two sources of structural ambiguity (e.g., prepositional phrase attachment, coordination scope) and explain why syntactic parsing must resolve them for accurate interpretation.

**Answer:** Two major sources of structural ambiguity are prepositional phrase (PP) attachment and coordination scope. PP attachment ambiguity occurs when a prepositional phrase can modify multiple constituents, as in "I saw the man with the telescope" where "with the telescope" can attach to either the verb phrase (I used a telescope to see) or the noun phrase (the man possessed a telescope). Coordination scope ambiguity arises when conjunctions like "and" or "or" can group constituents in multiple ways, such as "old men and women" which could mean "[old men] and [women]" or "old [men and women]". Syntactic parsing must resolve these ambiguities because different structural interpretations lead to fundamentally different semantic meanings—failing to choose the correct attachment or coordination scope results in misunderstanding speaker intent, incorrect semantic role assignment, and downstream errors in applications like machine translation, question answering, and information extraction where accurate meaning representation is critical.

- **(B)** Compare how phrase structure grammar and dependency grammar represent ambiguous constructions and discuss implications for computational parsing.

Phrase structure grammar represents ambiguous constructions through multiple distinct parse trees with different hierarchical bracketings—for "I saw the man with the telescope," one tree attaches the PP to VP while another attaches it to NP, explicitly encoding structural alternatives through different constituent arrangements. Dependency grammar represents the same ambiguity through different head-dependent relations—the preposition "with" can depend on either "saw" (verb attachment) or "man" (noun attachment)—creating competing dependency arcs rather than competing tree structures. For computational parsing, this distinction has significant implications: phrase structure parsers must search through exponentially many tree configurations and require disambiguation mechanisms like probabilistic scoring (PCFGs) to rank alternatives, while dependency parsers can leverage more efficient graph-based or transition-based algorithms that directly predict head-dependent relations, often achieving better computational complexity (O(n³) for many dependency parsers vs. higher complexity for exhaustive phrase structure parsing) and making them more practical for large-scale applications, though phrase structure representations may better capture certain linguistic phenomena like discontinuous constituents and explicit phrase boundaries.

#### Question 3: Dependency Parsing and Neural Models

- **(A)** Explain how dependency grammar represents syntactic structure as a tree of word-to-word relations and contrast it with constituency-based representations.

**Answer:** Dependency grammar represents syntactic structure as a directed tree where nodes are words from the sentence and edges (labeled with grammatical relations like "subject," "object," "modifier") directly connect a head word to its dependents, capturing functional relationships without intermediate phrase-level constituents—for example, in "John quickly ate pizza," "ate" serves as the root with "John" as its subject dependent, "quickly" as an adverbial modifier, and "pizza" as its object. This contrasts sharply with constituency-based representations, which organize words into hierarchical nested phrases (NP, VP, PP) that group words into multi-word constituents before showing how those constituents combine—the same sentence would be bracketed as "[S [NP John] [VP [Adv quickly] [V ate] [NP pizza]]]". The key difference is that dependency grammar is lexicentric (every node is a word with direct word-to-word relations) and produces a simpler, more linguistically transparent structure that directly encodes predicate-argument relationships, while constituency grammar is phrase-centric and produces deeper trees with abstract non-terminal nodes, making dependency representations often more efficient for computational parsing and more suitable for typologically diverse languages where phrase structure is less clear.

- **(B)** Describe how modern deep learning–based parsers use annotated treebanks and neural architectures to predict syntactic structure, and analyze the advantages of data-driven parsing over rule-based grammar systems.

**Answer:** Modern deep learning-based parsers use annotated treebanks (large corpora of sentences with gold-standard parse trees) to train neural architectures—such as BiLSTM encoders with attention mechanisms or Transformer-based models—that learn to predict syntactic structure by encoding input sentences into contextualized representations and then scoring or directly predicting parse decisions (e.g., which word is the head of each token, or transition actions in a shift-reduce parser), with parameters optimized end-to-end via backpropagation to maximize parsing accuracy on training examples. The advantages of data-driven parsing over rule-based grammar systems are substantial: (1) scalability—neural parsers automatically learn linguistic patterns from data rather than requiring linguists to hand-craft thousands of grammar rules, making them adaptable to new languages and domains with minimal expert effort; (2) robustness—they gracefully handle noisy, ungrammatical, or ambiguous input by learning statistical preferences from real-world text rather than relying on brittle symbolic rules that fail on unexpected constructions; (3) coverage—they can parse any input sentence (even if ungrammatical) by learning soft probabilistic patterns rather than hard constraints, avoiding the coverage gaps that plague rule-based systems when encountering constructions not anticipated by grammar writers; and (4) disambiguation—neural models implicitly learn to resolve ambiguity through contextualized representations and global scoring rather than needing explicit hand-coded disambiguation heuristics, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy (>95% on standard benchmarks) through data-driven pattern recognition that captures nuanced syntactic preferences impossible to encode manually.

### Language Modeling Foundations

#### Question 1: Probabilistic Foundations of Language Modeling

- **(A)** Formally define a language model as a conditional probability distribution and explain how it assigns probabilities to entire word sequences.

**Answer:** A language model is a probability distribution P(w₁, w₂, ..., wₙ) over sequences of words that assigns likelihoods to entire word sequences, typically factorized using the chain rule as P(w₁, w₂, ..., wₙ) = P(w₁) × P(w₂|w₁) × P(w₃|w₁,w₂) × ... × P(wₙ|w₁,...,wₙ₋₁), where each term represents the conditional probability of the next word given all preceding context. This formulation enables the model to capture sequential dependencies and assign higher probabilities to fluent, grammatical sequences while penalizing unlikely or ungrammatical word combinations—for example, P("the cat sat") would be higher than P("cat the sat")—making language modeling fundamentally about learning the joint distribution over word sequences that reflects natural language patterns observed in training corpora.

- **(B)** Analyze why language modeling is foundational for NLP applications and how probabilistic modeling supports ambiguity resolution and sequence comparison.

**Answer:** Language modeling is foundational for NLP because it provides a principled probabilistic framework for evaluating and generating text, serving as the backbone for tasks like machine translation (scoring translation candidates), speech recognition (disambiguating acoustically similar phrases), text generation (sampling coherent continuations), and spelling correction (ranking correction hypotheses). The probabilistic formulation directly addresses language's inherent ambiguity by assigning graded likelihoods to competing interpretations—for instance, in speech recognition, P("recognize speech"|acoustic signal) vs P("wreck a nice beach"|acoustic signal) can be compared to select the more probable transcription—while sequence comparison tasks like machine translation can rank alternative outputs by their probability under the model, enabling data-driven disambiguation without hand-coded rules and providing uncertainty quantification that supports downstream decision-making in complex NLP pipelines.

#### Question 2: n-Gram Models and Their Limitations

- **(A)** Explain the Markov assumption underlying n-gram language models and describe how n-gram probabilities are estimated from corpus counts.

**Answer:** The Markov assumption underlying n-gram models posits that the probability of a word depends only on the previous n-1 words rather than the entire preceding context, simplifying P(wₜ|w₁,...,wₜ₋₁) to P(wₜ|wₜ₋ₙ₊₁,...,wₜ₋₁), which makes estimation tractable by reducing the conditioning context to a fixed window (e.g., bigrams use n=2, considering only the immediate previous word). n-Gram probabilities are estimated using maximum likelihood estimation from corpus counts: P(wₜ|wₜ₋ₙ₊₁,...,wₜ₋₁) = count(wₜ₋ₙ₊₁,...,wₜ)/count(wₜ₋ₙ₊₁,...,wₜ₋₁), where counts are obtained by sliding an n-word window over the training corpus and tallying how often each n-gram appears—for example, in "the cat sat", a bigram model would count occurrences of "the cat" and "cat sat" to estimate P(cat|the) and P(sat|cat), making the approach purely count-based and directly reflecting observed word co-occurrence frequencies.

- **(B)** Analyze the sparsity and storage problems in high-order n-gram models, and discuss how smoothing, backoff, and interpolation partially address these issues.

**Answer:** High-order n-gram models suffer from severe sparsity because most possible n-grams never appear in training data (even with billions of tokens, the number of possible 5-grams with a 50k vocabulary is 50000⁵ ≈ 10²³, vastly exceeding observable instances), causing zero-probability assignments to unseen but valid sequences and making probability estimates unreliable, while storage requirements explode exponentially with n since models must store counts for all observed n-grams, requiring gigabytes of memory even for moderate vocabulary sizes. Smoothing techniques like add-k smoothing or Kneser-Ney smoothing address sparsity by redistributing probability mass from seen n-grams to unseen ones, ensuring non-zero probabilities for all sequences; backoff methods like Katz backoff handle unseen n-grams by recursively falling back to lower-order models (if a trigram is unseen, use the bigram estimate); and interpolation linearly combines predictions from multiple n-gram orders (e.g., P = λ₃P₃ + λ₂P₂ + λ₁P₁) to leverage both specific high-order patterns and robust low-order statistics, though these techniques only partially mitigate the fundamental limitation that count-based models cannot generalize beyond observed sequences and still require substantial storage for high-order n-grams.

#### Question 3: From Count-Based to Neural Language Models

- **(A)** Explain why n-gram models struggle to generalize beyond observed word sequences and how this limitation affects text generation quality.

**Answer:** n-Gram models struggle to generalize beyond observed sequences because they rely exclusively on exact string matching of word sequences—if "the black cat sat" appears in training but "the white cat sat" does not, the model assigns zero probability to the latter despite their semantic similarity, since count-based methods have no mechanism to recognize that "black" and "white" are similar color adjectives that should permit analogous constructions. This limitation severely affects text generation quality by producing brittle, repetitive output that either directly copies training n-grams (leading to memorization and lack of creativity) or assigns implausibly low probabilities to novel but perfectly grammatical and meaningful sequences, causing generated text to favor frequent but potentially generic phrases over more expressive alternatives simply because the model cannot interpolate between semantically related words or compositionally construct probabilities for unseen but valid combinations.

- **(B)** Describe how fixed-window neural language models address sparsity and storage constraints, and analyze how continuous representations enable generalization beyond exact counts.

**Answer:** Fixed-window neural language models address sparsity and storage constraints by replacing discrete count tables with learned continuous embeddings and neural network parameters: instead of storing counts for millions of distinct n-grams, the model learns a relatively compact weight matrix (typically thousands to millions of parameters) that maps concatenated word embeddings from a fixed context window through non-linear transformations to predict the next word, dramatically reducing storage from exponential in vocabulary size to linear in embedding dimension and network width. Continuous representations enable generalization beyond exact counts because semantically similar words have similar embeddings ("black" and "white" have nearby vectors), allowing the network to interpolate—if the model learns that "the black cat sat" is probable by updating weights for the embedding of "black", those same weights will naturally assign non-zero (and often high) probability to "the white cat sat" because "white"'s embedding activates similar hidden units, effectively enabling the model to recognize semantic patterns and compositional structure rather than memorizing exact string matches, thus generating fluent text even for word combinations never observed during training.

### Recurrent Neural Networks

#### Question 1: Fixed-Window vs Recurrent Language Models

- **(A)** Explain the limitations of fixed-window language models in capturing long-range dependencies.

**Answer:** Fixed-window language models are fundamentally constrained by their predetermined context size (e.g., 5-10 words), making them unable to capture dependencies that extend beyond this fixed horizon—for instance, subject-verb agreement in "The keys to the cabinet that I bought yesterday _are_ missing" requires tracking the plural subject "keys" across multiple intervening phrases, but a small window would only see "yesterday are" and miss the critical long-range dependency. Additionally, the fixed window size means the model cannot adaptively allocate attention to relevant context at different distances, treats all positions within the window uniformly despite varying relevance, and requires exponentially growing parameter counts to increase context size since the concatenated embedding dimension scales linearly with window length, making it computationally infeasible to extend context to dozens or hundreds of words where many linguistic phenomena (coreference, discourse coherence, thematic consistency) operate.

- **(B)** Describe how recurrent neural networks address these limitations through hidden-state recurrence and analyze how this changes the conditional probability formulation of language modeling.

**Answer:** Recurrent neural networks address fixed-window limitations by maintaining a hidden state vector h*t that is recursively updated at each timestep via h_t = f(h*{t-1}, x*t), where the hidden state compresses all preceding context into a fixed-dimensional representation that evolves dynamically as the sequence is processed, theoretically enabling unlimited context length without parameter growth. This changes the conditional probability formulation from P(w_t | w*{t-n+1}, ..., w*{t-1}) with fixed n-gram context to P(w_t | h*{t-1}), where h*{t-1} implicitly encodes the entire history w_1, ..., w*{t-1} through recursive composition, allowing the model to maintain long-range dependencies (subject-verb agreement, anaphora resolution) in principle while using constant memory and parameters—though in practice, gradient propagation challenges limit effective dependency length to tens rather than hundreds of timesteps.

#### Question 2: RNN Architecture and Sequence Probability Modeling

- **(A)** Describe how an RNN updates its hidden state at each timestep and how this state summarizes prior context.

**Answer:** An RNN updates its hidden state at each timestep t using h*t = tanh(W_h h*{t-1} + W*x x_t + b), where x_t is the current word embedding, h*{t-1} is the previous hidden state, and W*h, W_x are learned weight matrices that control how previous context and current input are combined through non-linear transformation. This hidden state h_t serves as a compressed summary of all prior context w_1, ..., w_t by recursively integrating information: at each step, the model blends the new word's contribution (W_x x_t) with the existing context summary (W_h h*{t-1}), effectively implementing a learned, differentiable form of sequential memory where the tanh activation prevents unbounded growth and the recurrent connections allow patterns from arbitrary timesteps in the past to influence the current representation, though in practice the contribution of very distant words decays exponentially due to repeated matrix multiplications during backpropagation.

- **(B)** Explain how an RNN language model computes conditional word probabilities and how this enables autoregressive text generation.

**Answer:** An RNN language model computes conditional word probabilities by projecting the hidden state h*t through an output layer P(w*{t+1} | w_1, ..., w_t) = softmax(W_out h_t + b_out), where the softmax normalizes scores across the entire vocabulary to produce a proper probability distribution over next words. This formulation enables autoregressive text generation by treating each generated word as the input for the next timestep: starting with an initial token (e.g., <START>), the model computes h_1, samples or selects the most probable next word w_1 from P(w_1), feeds w_1's embedding as x_2 to compute h_2 and P(w_2 | w_1), and continues iteratively until generating an end token or reaching maximum length, with each step conditioning on all previously generated tokens through the hidden state's recursive context compression, allowing the model to produce coherent multi-sentence text that maintains long-range thematic and syntactic consistency.

#### Question 3: Training and Backpropagation Through Time

- **(A)** Explain why training RNN language models requires Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) and describe how the network is conceptually unrolled.

**Answer:** Training RNN language models requires Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) because the recurrent connections create temporal dependencies where the loss at timestep t depends on hidden states from all previous timesteps, making standard backpropagation insufficient since it doesn't account for these sequential dependencies. BPTT conceptually unrolls the RNN across the sequence into a deep feedforward network where each timestep becomes a layer sharing the same parameters (W_h, W_x): for a sequence of length T, the unrolled network has T layers with identical weights, and gradients are computed by treating this as a standard deep network and applying the chain rule backward from the loss at each timestep through all preceding layers, accumulating gradients for the shared parameters across all timesteps before updating them—essentially, BPTT applies backpropagation to the unrolled computational graph, allowing the model to learn how hidden state transformations at each timestep contribute to prediction errors.

- **(B)** Analyze why gradient propagation across many timesteps creates optimization challenges and discuss the computational implications of parameter reuse across time.

**Answer:** Gradient propagation across many timesteps creates optimization challenges because repeated multiplication of the recurrent weight matrix W_h during backpropagation causes gradients to either vanish (when eigenvalues of W_h < 1, gradients shrink exponentially as they flow backward) or explode (when eigenvalues > 1, gradients grow exponentially), making it difficult to learn dependencies longer than 10-20 timesteps since the gradient signal from distant timesteps becomes too small to drive meaningful updates or too large to permit stable optimization. Parameter reuse across time means that the same weights W_h are used at every timestep, which has dual computational implications: (1) it enables constant memory complexity regardless of sequence length since the model doesn't need separate parameters for each position, but (2) it causes gradient accumulation where ∂L/∂W_h sums contributions from all timesteps, leading to high-variance gradients that can destabilize training and requiring careful techniques like gradient clipping (to prevent explosion), truncated BPTT (limiting backpropagation depth), and architectural modifications like LSTMs or GRUs that use gating mechanisms to create more stable gradient pathways and mitigate vanishing gradients.

### Sequence-to-Sequence Models

#### Question 1: Conditional Language Modeling and Encoder–Decoder Architecture

- **(A)** Formally define sequence-to-sequence learning as conditional language modeling and explain how the output sequence probability is factorized.

**Answer:** Sequence-to-sequence learning is formalized as conditional language modeling where the goal is to model P(y*1, ..., y_m | x_1, ..., x_n), the probability of an output sequence y conditioned on an input sequence x, which is factorized using the chain rule as P(y | x) = ∏*{t=1}^m P(y*t | y_1, ..., y*{t-1}, x_1, ..., x_n), decomposing the joint probability into a product of conditional distributions where each output token is predicted given all previous output tokens and the entire input sequence. This formulation treats output generation as autoregressive conditional prediction, where the model learns to sequentially predict the next output token while conditioning on both the input context and the partial output generated so far, enabling applications like machine translation (P(French sentence | English sentence)), summarization (P(summary | document)), and dialogue (P(response | conversation history)), with training maximizing the log-likelihood of observed output sequences given their corresponding inputs.

- **(B)** Describe the encoder–decoder architecture and analyze why compressing the entire input sequence into a single fixed-length context vector creates an information bottleneck.

**Answer:** The encoder-decoder architecture consists of two RNNs: an encoder that processes the input sequence x_1, ..., x_n to produce a final hidden state c (the context vector) that summarizes the entire input, and a decoder RNN that initializes with c and autoregressively generates the output sequence y_1, ..., y_m by conditioning each prediction on c and previous outputs. Compressing the entire input into a single fixed-length context vector creates an information bottleneck because c must encode arbitrarily long and complex input sequences into a fixed-dimensional vector (typically 512-1024 dimensions), causing inevitable information loss—long documents, nuanced semantic details, and specific source tokens cannot all be preserved in this limited representation, leading to degraded performance on long sequences where the encoder's final state prioritizes recent tokens due to recency bias while forgetting critical information from early in the sequence, and making it impossible for the decoder to selectively attend to relevant parts of the input at different generation steps since it only has access to the single aggregated summary c rather than the full sequence of encoder hidden states.

#### Question 2: Architectural Extensions in Seq2Seq Models

- **(A)** Explain the motivation for bidirectional RNNs and stacked (multi-layer) RNNs in encoder–decoder models.

**Answer:** Bidirectional RNNs are motivated by the observation that understanding a word in context requires information from both preceding and following tokens—for example, disambiguating "bank" in "river bank" requires seeing "river" before it—so the encoder processes the input sequence in both forward (left-to-right) and backward (right-to-left) directions, concatenating the two hidden states at each position to create representations that incorporate full sentential context rather than just prefix information. Stacked (multi-layer) RNNs stack multiple recurrent layers vertically, where each layer takes the hidden states from the layer below as input, enabling hierarchical representation learning where lower layers capture surface patterns (syntax, local phrases) while higher layers abstract to semantic and discourse-level features, analogous to how deep feedforward networks learn increasingly abstract features—this depth allows encoder-decoder models to construct richer, more compositional representations of input sequences that improve translation quality and generalization, though at the cost of increased parameters and training complexity.

- **(B)** Analyze how attention mechanisms address limitations of fixed-length representations and improve decoding for long input sequences.

**Answer:** Attention mechanisms address the fixed-length bottleneck by allowing the decoder to dynamically access the full sequence of encoder hidden states rather than relying on a single context vector: at each decoding timestep t, attention computes alignment scores between the current decoder state and all encoder states, normalizes them via softmax to produce attention weights α_ti, and creates a context vector c_t = Σ_i α_ti h_i^enc that adaptively focuses on relevant input positions for predicting the current output token. This eliminates information loss because the decoder can directly retrieve information from any input position rather than having it compressed through the encoder's final state, and improves long sequence handling by enabling the model to maintain consistent access to source tokens throughout generation—for example, in translating a long sentence, the decoder can attend strongly to the source subject when generating the target subject regardless of sentence length, effectively creating dynamic, token-specific "soft pointers" into the input that overcome the memory limitations of fixed-dimensional context vectors and enable accurate translation of sentences with hundreds of tokens.

#### Question 3: Neural Machine Translation and Evaluation

- **(A)** Explain how neural machine translation is formulated as a sequence-to-sequence task and describe the role of attention in improving translation quality.

**Answer:** Neural machine translation is formulated as sequence-to-sequence learning by modeling P(target sentence | source sentence), where an encoder RNN processes the source language input (e.g., English) into contextual representations and a decoder RNN autoregressively generates the target language output (e.g., French) by predicting each target token conditioned on the encoded source and previously generated target tokens, with the entire model trained end-to-end to maximize the probability of reference translations. Attention dramatically improves translation quality by enabling the decoder to dynamically focus on relevant source words when generating each target word—for instance, when translating "the black cat" to "le chat noir," attention allows the decoder to strongly weight "black" when generating "noir" despite word order differences, effectively learning soft word alignments that handle non-monotonic translation patterns, long-distance dependencies, and divergent syntactic structures that would be poorly captured by a fixed context vector, resulting in more accurate, fluent translations especially for long sentences and morphologically complex languages.

- **(B)** Compare automatic evaluation metrics such as perplexity and BLEU with human evaluation, and analyze challenges in assessing Seq2Seq model performance.

**Answer:** Automatic metrics like perplexity (measuring how well the model predicts held-out sequences, with lower values indicating better probability assignments) and BLEU (computing n-gram overlap between generated and reference translations, with higher scores indicating greater lexical similarity) provide fast, reproducible evaluation but suffer critical limitations: perplexity only assesses probability calibration without measuring actual output quality, while BLEU's reliance on surface-form matching fails to credit semantically equivalent paraphrases, penalizes valid alternative translations, and correlates poorly with human judgments for individual sentences. Human evaluation, which asks annotators to rate translation adequacy (semantic completeness) and fluency (grammaticality), directly measures communicative success but is expensive, subjective, time-consuming, and difficult to reproduce, while facing challenges like inter-annotator disagreement, lack of standardization across studies, and inability to provide fine-grained diagnostic feedback about specific model failures—ultimately, comprehensive Seq2Seq evaluation requires combining automatic metrics for rapid iteration with targeted human evaluation to validate that models achieve genuine communicative competence rather than merely optimizing surface-level statistical proxies.

### Convolutional Neural Networks for NLP

#### Question 1: Structural Limitations of RNNs and Motivation for CNNs

- **(A)** Explain the structural limitations of RNNs in modeling phrase-level meaning, including prefix dependency, recency bias, and final-step prediction bottlenecks.

**Answer:** RNNs suffer from structural limitations in modeling phrase-level meaning due to their sequential processing: prefix dependency means the representation of "black cat" at position t depends on having already processed "black" at t-1, making it impossible to capture phrase meanings in parallel and causing the model to incrementally update rather than directly compose constituents; recency bias results from the gradual decay of information through repeated hidden state transformations, making recent words disproportionately influential while distant words (even within the same phrase) fade, so "very very very very happy" biases toward the last "very" rather than treating all modifiers equally; and final-step prediction bottlenecks occur because sentence classification typically uses only the last hidden state h_T, forcing all semantic information to funnel through this single vector and potentially losing important phrase-level patterns that appeared earlier in the sequence but weren't preserved through the recurrent updates.

- **(B)** Analyze why these limitations motivate the use of convolutional architectures for certain NLP tasks.

**Answer:** These RNN limitations motivate CNNs for NLP tasks because convolutional architectures operate on local windows of consecutive words in parallel rather than sequentially, directly capturing phrase-level patterns like "not good" or "very happy" through filters that detect specific n-gram features without prefix dependency—this parallel computation enables faster training through better GPU utilization, avoids recency bias by treating all positions within a filter window equally, and uses pooling operations (max/average pooling) to extract salient phrase-level features from anywhere in the sentence rather than relying solely on final hidden states, making CNNs particularly effective for tasks like sentiment analysis and sentence classification where local phrases ("excellent service," "terrible quality") carry strong predictive signals and where capturing compositional meaning of short contiguous spans matters more than modeling very long-range sequential dependencies.

#### Question 2: Convolution and Pooling in Text CNNs

- **(A)** Formally describe how 1D convolution operates over word embeddings to produce feature maps, including the roles of window size, stride, padding, and channels.

**Answer:** 1D convolution operates over a sequence of word embeddings by sliding a filter (a learnable weight matrix of size k×d, where k is the window size and d is the embedding dimension) across the input, computing at each position i a feature value  f*i = activation(W · x*{i:i+k-1} + b) where x\_{i:i+k-1} is the concatenation of k consecutive word embeddings and W is the filter—window size k determines the n-gram span captured (k=3 detects trigrams like "not very good"), stride controls how many positions the filter shifts between applications (stride=1 for dense overlapping windows, stride>1 for downsampling), padding adds special tokens at sequence boundaries to preserve edge information and control output length, and channels represent multiple parallel filters that extract different features (e.g., 100 channels learn 100 distinct phrase patterns), with the result being a feature map of shape (sequence_length - k + 1, num_channels) that captures phrase-level patterns at all positions.

- **(B)** Compare max pooling, average pooling, and k-max pooling, and analyze how each affects sentence-level representation.

**Answer:** Max pooling takes the maximum activation value across each feature map (one value per filter), creating a fixed-size representation that captures whether a particular pattern appeared anywhere in the sentence regardless of position, emphasizing the most salient phrase match and providing translation invariance but losing all positional and frequency information. Average pooling computes the mean activation across positions, producing a more moderate aggregate that reflects how prevalent a pattern is throughout the sentence and is less sensitive to outliers, but may dilute strong local signals by averaging with irrelevant positions. k-max pooling selects the top k activation values per feature map (preserving their order), retaining information about multiple important phrase matches and their relative positions, which helps capture patterns that appear multiple times or in specific orders ("not good but not terrible") while still achieving some dimensionality reduction—max pooling works well for binary detection tasks ("does this review mention excellent service?"), average pooling suits holistic sentiment aggregation, while k-max pooling balances between preserving structural information and achieving fixed-length representations suitable for downstream classification.

#### Question 3: CNNs for Sentence Classification and Architectural Trade-offs

- **(A)** Describe the standard CNN architecture for sentence classification, including convolutional filters of varying widths, pooling, fully connected layers, and output activation functions.

**Answer:** The standard CNN architecture for sentence classification starts with an input embedding layer that maps words to dense vectors, followed by a convolutional layer with multiple filters of varying widths (typically k=2,3,4,5) to capture different n-gram patterns—100 filters of size 3 detect trigrams, 100 filters of size 4 detect 4-grams, etc.—producing separate feature maps for each filter that detect specific phrase patterns at all positions in the sentence. Max-over-time pooling is then applied to each feature map independently, selecting the maximum activation per filter to create a fixed-length feature vector (e.g., 400 values if using 100 filters each of sizes 2,3,4,5) that represents the most salient phrase patterns detected anywhere in the input, regardless of sentence length. This pooled representation is passed through one or more fully connected (dense) layers with dropout regularization to learn non-linear combinations of phrase features, and finally through an output layer with softmax activation (for multi-class classification) or sigmoid (for binary/multi-label) that produces probability distributions over target classes, enabling end-to-end training via backpropagation with cross-entropy loss.

- **(B)** Compare CNNs with RNNs and Transformers in terms of phrase modeling, computational efficiency, and ability to capture long-range dependencies.

**Answer:** CNNs excel at phrase modeling through local receptive fields that directly capture n-gram patterns ("not good," "very happy") with position-invariant filters, are computationally efficient with O(n) complexity and high parallelization since all filter applications at all positions can be computed simultaneously on GPUs, but struggle with long-range dependencies beyond their maximum filter width since a size-5 filter can only see 5-word windows, requiring deep stacking to expand receptive fields. RNNs naturally model sequential dependencies through recurrent hidden states that theoretically encode unlimited history, handle variable-length sequences gracefully, and can capture long-range patterns in principle, but suffer from O(n) sequential bottlenecks that prevent parallelization (each step depends on the previous), vanishing gradients that limit effective context to ~20 tokens in practice, and recency bias that prioritizes recent over distant words. Transformers via self-attention achieve O(1) depth to any position pair (every token attends to every other token directly), enable full parallelization across sequence length for O(n²) per-layer complexity, capture both local phrase patterns and global long-range dependencies simultaneously, and have become dominant for modern NLP—ultimately, CNNs remain useful for scenarios requiring extreme efficiency and local pattern detection, RNNs for online processing and very long sequences with limited compute, while Transformers represent the current state-of-the-art for most tasks where computational resources permit their quadratic attention cost.

### Advanced CNN Architectures

#### Question 1: Model Comparisons and Multi-Channel CNNs

- **(A)** Compare Bag-of-Vectors models, Window Models, CNNs, and RNNs in terms of structural assumptions, computational properties, and ability to model order and context.

**Answer:** Bag-of-Vectors models (averaging or summing word embeddings) assume order-independence and uniform word importance, are computationally trivial with O(n) time, and completely discard sequential structure, making them suitable only for tasks where word presence matters more than syntax (e.g., topic classification); Window Models capture local context within fixed windows but treat each window independently with no interaction across windows, require O(n) computation with parallelization, and model limited positional patterns within their window size but miss global structure; CNNs use convolutional filters to detect phrase-level patterns across the entire sequence with weight sharing and position invariance, achieve O(n) complexity with high parallelizability, and capture local compositional patterns (n-grams) hierarchically through stacking but struggle with very long-range dependencies; RNNs process sequences recursively with hidden states that theoretically encode complete history, have O(n) sequential complexity that prevents parallelization, model arbitrary-length dependencies in principle but suffer vanishing gradients and recency bias in practice—essentially, the progression from Bag-of-Vectors → Window Models → CNNs → RNNs represents increasing sophistication in modeling sequential structure at the cost of computational complexity and training difficulty.

- **(B)** Explain how multi-channel CNNs enhance feature representation and analyze how parallel embedding channels contribute complementary information.

**Answer:** Multi-channel CNNs enhance feature representation by using multiple parallel embedding channels as input, analogous to RGB color channels in computer vision—typically one channel contains static pretrained embeddings (like Word2Vec or GloVe) that provide stable, general-purpose semantic representations, while another channel contains task-specific fine-tuned embeddings that adapt to domain-specific patterns, and optionally a third channel with character-level or positional encodings. Separate convolutional filters operate over each channel independently, and their outputs are concatenated or combined before pooling, allowing the model to simultaneously leverage pretrained knowledge (static channel captures that "excellent" and "great" are similar) and task-specific adaptations (fine-tuned channel learns that "not bad" is positive in movie reviews despite individual word polarities), while different channels can capture complementary aspects like syntax (pretrained embeddings encode grammatical patterns), semantics (fine-tuned embeddings emphasize task-relevant meaning distinctions), and morphology (character-level channels handle misspellings and rare words)—this multi-view learning enables more robust representations than single-channel architectures by combining stable general features with adaptive specialized features.

#### Question 2: Vertical Gating and Optimization in Deep Architectures

- **(A)** Explain the concept of vertical gating and describe how residual connections and highway networks stabilize deep neural architectures.

**Answer:** Vertical gating refers to mechanisms that control information flow between layers in deep networks through learnable gates that decide how much to transform versus pass through input representations. Residual connections (ResNets) implement this via h_out = h_in + F(h_in), where F is a learned transformation and the identity shortcut (+h_in) creates a direct gradient pathway that allows errors to flow backward through many layers without degradation, enabling training of networks with hundreds of layers by ensuring that each layer only needs to learn residual adjustments rather than complete transformations. Highway networks extend this with learned gating: h_out = T·F(h_in) + (1-T)·h_in, where T = σ(W_T h_in) is a transform gate that dynamically decides how much to apply the learned transformation versus copy the input, allowing the network to adaptively create shortcuts when deep transformations aren't beneficial—both mechanisms prevent vanishing gradients, enable stable optimization of very deep architectures, and allow lower layers to pass information directly to higher layers when intermediate transformations would be harmful.

- **(B)** Analyze the role of Batch Normalization in mitigating internal covariate shift and enabling efficient training of very deep networks.

**Answer:** Batch Normalization mitigates internal covariate shift—the problem where the distribution of layer inputs changes during training as parameters in previous layers update, forcing each layer to constantly adapt to shifting input statistics—by normalizing activations within each mini-batch to have zero mean and unit variance before applying learned scale and shift parameters (γ, β), which stabilizes the optimization landscape by ensuring consistent input distributions across training iterations. This enables efficient training of very deep networks by reducing sensitivity to initialization and learning rates (normalized activations prevent exploding/vanishing values), allowing much higher learning rates that accelerate convergence, acting as implicit regularization that reduces overfitting (the batch statistics introduce noise similar to dropout), and smoothing the loss landscape so gradient descent finds better minima more reliably—empirically, Batch Normalization allows training networks with 50-100+ layers that would otherwise fail to converge, and has become a standard component in modern deep architectures for both computer vision and NLP, though its effectiveness comes partly from smoothing the optimization surface rather than only addressing covariate shift as originally hypothesized.

#### Question 3: Very Deep CNNs, QRNNs, and Architectural Evolution

- **(A)** Describe the design of Very Deep CNNs (VD-CNN) for text processing and explain how depth substitutes for recurrence in hierarchical feature construction.

**Answer:** Very Deep CNNs (VD-CNN) apply convolutional architectures inspired by computer vision (VGGNet, ResNet) to text by stacking many convolutional layers (up to 29 layers) with small filter sizes (typically 3) and pooling operations that progressively downsample sequence length while increasing feature dimensions, operating directly on character-level inputs to learn representations from scratch without pretrained embeddings. Depth substitutes for recurrence by enabling hierarchical feature construction through layer composition: lower layers detect character patterns and morphemes ("ing", "un-"), middle layers compose these into word-level features and local phrases, and higher layers capture sentence and document-level semantics—each additional convolutional layer expands the effective receptive field, so a 10-layer network with size-3 filters can integrate information across ~20 positions, approximating the long-range context modeling of RNNs while maintaining parallelizability and avoiding vanishing gradient issues. This demonstrates that with sufficient depth, feedforward convolutional architectures can achieve competitive performance on text classification and language modeling through learned hierarchical abstractions rather than explicit sequential recurrence.

- **(B)** Explain the motivation behind Quasi-Recurrent Neural Networks (QRNNs) and analyze their trade-offs compared to traditional RNNs and attention-based models.

**Answer:** Quasi-Recurrent Neural Networks (QRNNs) are motivated by the desire to combine CNNs' computational efficiency and parallelizability with RNNs' ability to model sequential dependencies: QRNNs apply convolutional filters across time to compute candidate values and gates in parallel (like CNNs), then use these gates to control a simplified recurrent pooling operation (similar to RNN hidden state updates but without matrix multiplications) that maintains sequential state. Compared to traditional RNNs, QRNNs achieve much faster training and inference through parallelized convolution while still capturing temporal dependencies through gating and pooling, reducing computation from O(nhd²) for RNNs to O(nkd²) where k is the small filter width, and enabling better GPU utilization—however, they sacrifice some modeling flexibility since the convolutional gates have limited receptive fields and cannot condition on arbitrarily distant context like full RNNs with hidden state recurrence. Compared to attention-based models (Transformers), QRNNs are more parameter-efficient and computationally cheaper (linear vs quadratic in sequence length) but less expressive since they lack the global token-to-token attention that enables Transformers to directly model long-range dependencies—ultimately, QRNNs represent an architectural middle ground optimized for fast sequence processing with moderate context modeling, suitable for resource-constrained scenarios where full attention is prohibitively expensive.

### Subword and Character-Level Modeling

#### Question 1: Motivation and Linguistic Foundations of Subword Modeling

- **(A)** Explain why treating words as atomic units creates limitations in open-vocabulary and morphologically rich languages.

**Answer:** Treating words as atomic units creates severe limitations because it requires predefined fixed vocabularies (typically 30k-50k words) that cannot handle unseen words (out-of-vocabulary or OOV terms), causing models to map all unknown words to a single <UNK> token and lose critical information—for example, encountering "COVID-19" or "cryptocurrency" after training results in complete loss of semantic content. This is especially problematic for morphologically rich languages like Turkish, Finnish, or German where productive morphology creates virtually infinite word forms through compounding and inflection ("Donaudampfschifffahrtsgesellschaft"), making it impossible to enumerate all valid words, and where related forms like "run"/"running"/"runner" are treated as completely independent despite sharing a root, preventing the model from leveraging morphological relationships and requiring separate embeddings for each variant, leading to data sparsity since training examples are fragmented across inflected forms rather than consolidated around shared meaning.

- **(B)** Analyze how character-level and subword-level modeling address out-of-vocabulary issues, morphological variation, and multilingual generalization.

**Answer:** Character-level modeling eliminates OOV issues entirely by representing text as sequences of characters (typically 100-200 unique symbols including letters, punctuation, and special characters) that can compose any word, enabling the model to handle novel words, misspellings, and domain-specific terminology by learning compositional patterns from character sequences—for instance, recognizing that words ending in "-ing" are likely verbs and "un-" prefixes indicate negation. Subword-level modeling (BPE, WordPiece, SentencePiece) strikes a balance by segmenting words into frequently occurring subword units ("playing" → "play" + "ing", "unhappiness" → "un" + "happiness") using data-driven algorithms, which addresses OOV issues (rare words decompose into known subwords), captures morphological regularities explicitly (all "-ing" forms share the same subword token), enables parameter sharing across related words ("run"/"running"/"runner" share the "run" subword), and improves multilingual generalization by identifying cross-lingual subword overlaps ("international" shares roots with "internacional"/"international" across languages)—ultimately, both approaches trade word-level semantic directness for improved coverage, compositional representation, and data efficiency, with subword methods achieving better practical balance than pure character models which require longer sequences and deeper networks to reconstruct word-level meaning.

#### Question 2: Subword Tokenization Algorithms and Their Trade-Offs

- **(A)** Compare Byte Pair Encoding (BPE), WordPiece, and the Unigram Language Model in terms of their optimization objectives and segmentation strategies.

**Answer:** Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) iteratively merges the most frequent character or subword pair in the corpus (starting with characters and greedily merging based on frequency counts) until reaching a target vocabulary size, using a deterministic, frequency-based approach that segments words by applying learned merge rules in order during inference. WordPiece, used by BERT, is similar to BPE but optimizes for maximum likelihood: instead of raw frequency, it merges pairs that most increase the probability of the training corpus under a language model, leading to segmentations that better reflect predictive utility rather than pure occurrence counts, and adds "##" markers to denote subword continuations ("playing" → "play" + "##ing"). The Unigram Language Model (used by SentencePiece) takes a probabilistic approach by starting with a large vocabulary and iteratively removing tokens whose removal least degrades the corpus likelihood, assigning probabilities to each token and using dynamic programming (Viterbi algorithm) to find the most probable segmentation during inference rather than applying deterministic merge rules—this probabilistic framework allows sampling multiple segmentations during training for regularization, while BPE and WordPiece produce deterministic single segmentations, making Unigram more flexible but computationally expensive.

- **(B)** Analyze the computational trade-offs between vocabulary size and sequence length in Transformer models, including implications for attention complexity and memory usage.

**Answer:** Vocabulary size and sequence length present a fundamental trade-off in Transformer models: larger vocabularies (50k-100k+ tokens) reduce sequence length by representing more content per token ("playing" as one token vs. "play"+"ing" as two), which decreases the quadratic attention cost O(n²d) where n is sequence length, but increases embedding table size O(Vd) where V is vocabulary size and d is embedding dimension, requiring more memory and making embedding lookups more expensive. Conversely, smaller vocabularies (10k-30k tokens) require longer sequences since words split into more subwords, exponentially increasing attention memory (storing the n×n attention matrix) and computation (computing pairwise attention scores for all token pairs), which becomes prohibitive for long documents—for example, halving vocabulary size might double sequence length, quadrupling attention cost from n² to (2n)²=4n². The optimal balance depends on typical input length and hardware constraints: for models like BERT handling 512-token contexts, larger vocabularies (~30k-50k) are preferred to minimize sequence length and attention cost, while very large language models with multi-thousand token contexts might use smaller vocabularies to reduce embedding parameters, accepting longer tokenized sequences—additionally, larger vocabularies reduce information density per token (more specific tokens like "COVID-19" vs. decomposed subwords) but risk increased sparsity since rare tokens receive fewer training examples, while smaller vocabularies force more compositional representation learning but at computational cost.

#### Question 3: Character-Level Modeling, Tokenizer Drift, and Scaling

- **(A)** Describe how pure character-level architectures construct representations and discuss their advantages and computational costs.

**Answer:** Pure character-level architectures construct representations by treating text as sequences of individual characters, typically using convolutional layers or RNNs/Transformers to progressively compose character-level features into word-level and phrase-level representations—for example, a CNN might use filters of increasing size (1-7 characters) to detect character n-grams, morphemes, and words hierarchically, or a Transformer encoder processes character sequences with self-attention before pooling to sentence representations. Advantages include complete elimination of OOV issues (any unicode character is representable), inherent handling of misspellings and morphological variation, language-agnostic processing requiring minimal preprocessing, and ability to learn subword patterns directly from data—however, computational costs are severe: sequences become 5-10× longer than word or subword tokenization ("playing" is 7 characters vs. 1-2 subwords), causing quadratic attention and memory costs to explode in Transformers, requiring much deeper networks to reconstruct word-level semantics from character compositions, increasing training time by orders of magnitude, and making character-level models impractical for long-document tasks despite their theoretical advantages, which is why hybrid approaches (character-aware embeddings feeding into word/subword models) or pure subword tokenization dominate modern NLP.

- **(B)** Explain the concept of tokenizer drift and evaluate how tokenization choices influence robustness, domain transfer, and scaling in modern large language models.

**Answer:** Tokenizer drift refers to the phenomenon where subword tokenization trained on one domain (e.g., general web text) produces suboptimal segmentations on different domains (e.g., medical, legal, code), causing domain-specific terms to fragment into many low-information pieces—for instance, "acetylsalicylic" might split into 6-7 generic subwords with a general tokenizer but could be a single token with domain-specific vocabulary, leading to inefficient sequence encoding, loss of semantic coherence, and degraded model performance since the model must learn to reconstruct domain meanings from uninformative fragments. Tokenization choices critically influence robustness (byte-level tokenization handles any text but produces longer sequences; word-level breaks on OOV), domain transfer (domain-specific tokenizers improve in-domain performance but hurt transfer; general tokenizers enable zero-shot transfer at the cost of in-domain efficiency), and scaling (larger vocabularies reduce sequence length enabling longer contexts but increase embedding parameters and sparsity; smaller vocabularies lengthen sequences increasing attention cost quadratically)—modern large language models increasingly use byte-level tokenization (treating UTF-8 bytes as tokens) or carefully constructed multilingual subword vocabularies to maximize coverage and transferability, accepting modest efficiency costs in exchange for universal applicability and robustness to noisy, multilingual, and code-mixed text that would severely degrade word-level or domain-specific tokenization approaches.
